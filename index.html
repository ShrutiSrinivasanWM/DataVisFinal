Introduction

The original NIH data consists of the 16S microbial sequencing results for 31 nasal cavity samples. For this project, I wanted to look at how gender affects the populations of the bacterial phyla present in nasal cavities, if at all. As a result, I am analyzing a subset of the provided data, using the relative abundances of these results to allow for a more accurate and helpful comparison of the various microbial species that exist in these samples. I also removed some of the more sparcely populated species in order to isolate the more abundant ones and allow for higher resolution visualizations. Therefore, I ended up with a subset of Actinobacteria, Bacteroidetes, Cyanobacteria, Firmicutes, Proteobacteria, and the categorical variable of sex in my dataset.

Heatmap

The heatmap shows the magnitude of relationships between the 5 bacterial phyla populations. Here we can see that Bacteroidetes and Actinobacteria have the greatest connection based on the absolute value of the percentage shown (~60%). This can be interpreted to mean that these two bacterial phyla tend to grow together or exist together in nasal cavities more than either of the do along with any of the other species. Although we cannot make an assumption of whether or not this correlates to gender classifications, the heatmap provides us with a decent starting point for future comparisons.
